\begin{frame}[shrink=6]{From Agents to PDEs: The Closure Problem}
\small

From microscopic trajectories on the periodic domain
$\Omega=[0,L_x)\times[0,L_y)$, the thesis observes the coarse-grained state
\[
  \mathbf{q}(x,t)
  =
  \bigl(\rho(x,t),\,p_x(x,t),\,p_y(x,t),\,\Phi(x,t)\bigr),
  \qquad
  \Phi = W * \rho .
\]

The density must satisfy a \textbf{conservation law}:
\[
  \partial_t \rho + \nabla\!\cdot \mathbf{j} = 0.
\]

The \textbf{closure problem} is to identify an \textbf{effective flux law}
\[
  \mathbf{j}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}[\rho,\mathbf{p},\Phi],
  \qquad
  \mathbf{p}=(p_x,p_y),
\]
such that
\[
  \partial_t \rho + \nabla\!\cdot
  \mathbf{j}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}[\rho,\mathbf{p},\Phi] = 0
\]
matches the observed field data.

\medskip
Scalar density alone is not expected to close: alignment survives in
$\mathbf{p}$ and Morse interactions survive in $\Phi$.
\end{frame}
