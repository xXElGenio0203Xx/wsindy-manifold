\chapter{Additional Robustness Studies}
\label{app:studies_in_progress}

This appendix collects two robustness studies assembled from Oscar
cluster results.  The noise sweep
(Section~\ref{app:noise_robustness}) tests identification and forecast
stability across varying noise amplitudes, while the particle-count
convergence study (Section~\ref{app:n_convergence}) verifies the
mean-field limit.

% ======================================================================
\section{Physical inductive biases}
\label{app:pipeline_fixes}
% ======================================================================

Section~\ref{sec:pipeline_fixes} describes six physical inductive biases
in full; a compact summary is reproduced here for self-contained
reference.

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]

  \item \textbf{Centred flux estimation.}
    The KDE flux estimator subtracts the domain-mean flux from each snapshot
    before convolution, preventing a spurious DC offset from aliasing into
    the linear drag coefficient.

  \item \textbf{Biharmonic operator removal.}
    The biharmonic ($\nabla^4$) library term was removed: it is not part of
    the expected mean-field structure and in trial runs absorbed energy from
    the viscous term, degrading interpretability.

  \item \textbf{Temporal test-function width floor.}
    The temporal support is floored at $\ell_t \geq 7$ grid points to prevent
    the width optimiser from collapsing the weak-form integral to a pointwise
    residual.

  \item \textbf{Sign constraints on post-fit coefficients.}
    After each MSTLS solve, coefficients whose physical sign is known
    \emph{a priori} (e.g.\ viscous terms must be non-positive) are checked;
    sign-violating survivors are zeroed and the fit re-solved.

  \item \textbf{Coefficient magnitude cap.}
    Retained coefficients exceeding $|c|>5.0$ are removed, guarding against
    inflation from near-singular library matrices.

  \item \textbf{Regime-aware library construction.}
    The \cv{forces.enabled} flag controls whether Morse potential terms enter
    the candidate library; for pure-Vicsek regimes the Morse columns are
    automatically excluded.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Noise robustness}
\label{app:noise_robustness}
% ======================================================================

The primary question is whether the \emph{same mechanisms remain
identifiable} under varying noise, rather than whether weak-form $R^2$
degrades.  We run the full pipeline at five noise levels for each of four
regimes: gas ($\eta\in\{0.15, 0.50, 1.00, 1.50, 2.00\}$, native
$\eta{=}1.5$), blackhole ($\eta\in\{0.05, 0.15, 0.50, 1.00, 1.50\}$,
native $\eta{=}0.15$), supernova ($\eta\in\{0.05, 0.15, 0.50, 1.00,
1.50\}$, native $\eta{=}0.15$), and crystal
($\eta\in\{0.05, 0.10, 0.50, 1.00, 1.50\}$, native $\eta{=}0.10$).
We focus the full pipeline (ROM + WSINDy) on the gas and blackhole
regimes, where the particle-level physics permits meaningful ROM
comparison.  For the supernova and crystal regimes, only WSINDy
identification is reported (Table~\ref{tab:noise_sweep_wsonly}).
Table~\ref{tab:noise_sweep_forecast} collects the gas and blackhole
results.

Across the gas sweep, MVAR remains above $R^2 \geq 0.993$ at all five
noise levels; LSTM degrades gracefully from $0.983$ at $\eta{=}1.00$ to
$0.806$ at $\eta{=}2.00$, consistent with the distribution-shift
sensitivity discussed in Section~\ref{sec:ch7_lstm_modes}.
For blackhole, MVAR stays above $0.983$ at all five noise levels
(including $0.992$ at the heaviest noise $\eta{=}1.50$),
while LSTM drops sharply to $0.125$ at $\eta{=}1.00$ and $0.251$ at
$\eta{=}0.50$, confirming that the black-hole's radial
attractor is harder for feed-forward LSTM rollouts.

WSINDy identification is robust in every completed noise run:
the continuity backbone $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{p}\approx -1$ is retained
wherever the Oscar jobs produced WSINDy summary files, and the density
weak-form fit remains above $0.998$ in all completed rows.
The blank WSINDy entries correspond to Oscar jobs that produced only
metadata directories and no recovered model files
($\eta{=}0.05$ and $0.15$ for blackhole, and $\eta{=}0.50$ for
supernova).
The sole visible degradation among the completed WSINDy runs appears in the
supernova regime, where $R^2_{\mathrm{wf},\rho}$ drops from $0.9998$
at the native level ($\eta{=}0.15$) to $0.9982$ at $\eta{=}1.50$
(Table~\ref{tab:noise_sweep_wsonly}),
consistent with the supernova's higher microscopic complexity
(Figure~\ref{fig:noise_coeff_stability}).

\section{Convergence in the number of particles}
\label{app:n_convergence}
% ======================================================================

To verify that the WSINDy-recovered PDEs represent a genuine mean-field
limit, we repeat the pure Vicsek identification at
$N\in\{50, 100, 200, 300, 500, 1000\}$.
Table~\ref{tab:n_convergence_forecast} reports the
companion MVAR and LSTM forecast quality alongside the WSINDy weak-form
$R^2$ where available.
The newly synced ROM runs extend the table to $N{=}500$ and $N{=}1000$:
at $N{=}500$, MVAR drops to mean $R^2{=}0.189$ while retaining
$R^2{=}0.992$ on the Gaussian-IC family, and LSTM diverges
(marked~$\dagger$); at $N{=}1000$, MVAR rebounds to $R^2{=}0.713$,
the Gaussian-IC score remains $0.992$, and LSTM stays below zero.
WSINDy results are available only for $N{=}50$--$300$;
at all four completed particle counts,
WSINDy recovers $R^2_{\text{wf},\rho}\geq 0.9999$ with a consistent
active set of $|\mathcal{A}|{=}5$ terms (one for $\rho$, two each for
$p_x$ and $p_y$); the reported $R^2_{\text{wf},p}$ values use the
$p_x$ equation, with $p_y$ showing comparable fits in all completed
runs.  Momentum fit ranges from $R^2_{\text{wf},p}{=}0.80$
to~$0.85$, suggesting the mean-field limit is well-approximated even at
moderate particle counts.

MVAR $R^2$ is stable at $0.54$--$0.65$ for $N{=}50$--$300$,
drops to $0.189$ at $N{=}500$, and recovers to $0.713$ at $N{=}1000$;
however, the Gaussian-IC
performance remains $R^2 > 0.99$ across all six tested counts,
indicating that the forecast ceiling is limited by IC diversity rather
than particle number.
LSTM diverges ($R^2 < 0$) from $N{=}200$ onward,
reflecting sensitivity to the growing latent state
dimension.  The $N{=}500$ run is the strongest failure
($R^2=-8.12$), while $N{=}1000$ remains below zero
($R^2=-0.34$); this trend is consistent
with the shift-predictability bottleneck discussed in the main text.

\paragraph{Summary of findings.}
Across both robustness studies, WSINDy identification proves remarkably
stable: the continuity backbone $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{p}\approx -1$ is
recovered in every completed WSINDy noise and particle-count run, with density
$R^2_{\mathrm{wf},\rho}\geq 0.9982$ in all completed runs.  The ROM
forecasting picture is more nuanced.  MVAR maintains $R^2>0.98$ on
Gaussian ICs throughout, but its mean-$R^2$ degrades on non-Gaussian
families---particularly for the blackhole attractor at high noise and for
pure Vicsek at $N{=}500$.  LSTM is the most fragile component: it
degrades gracefully in the gas regime but diverges for the blackhole
at $\eta\geq 0.50$ and for pure Vicsek at $N\geq 200$.
These results confirm the main-text conclusion
(Section~\ref{sec:ch7_lstm_modes}) that MVAR is the more reliable ROM,
while LSTM's expressivity becomes a liability when the latent dynamics
shift away from the training distribution.
